# Discovery Guide (Phase 0)

The intake conversation is what separates a good result from a generic one. This guide details how to run it.

## Read the emotional core

Before showing anything visual, tell the user what their app IS emotionally. Method:

1. Look past the feature list to the **payoff moment** — the single instant the product exists to deliver.
2. Name the **feeling** at that moment in plain words ("recognition", "relief", "pride", "delight").
3. Reframe the whole product around it. "This isn't an X tool, it's a Y tool."

This does two things: it proves you understand the product, and it gives every later design choice a north star. A color or font is "right" only relative to a feeling.

## Name the central tension

Most apps carry one design tension that the visuals must resolve. Examples:
- Serious/technical engine vs. warm/human payoff
- Playful concept vs. trustworthy money-handling
- Dense data vs. calm clarity

State it explicitly. It tells you what to be careful about.

## The two calibration questions

Ask these as tappable single-select questions. They collapse the design space fast.

**Q1 — What should the payoff moment FEEL like?**
Options are emotion words tuned to the app, e.g.:
- Warm recognition ("someone gets me")
- Delightful surprise ("whoa, no way")
- Calm & credible ("I trust this")
- Motivating ("I can do this now")

**Q2 — Who should the app feel like it was made by?**
- A thoughtful indie maker (personal, crafted)
- A serious research/AI lab (precise, smart)
- A friendly learning/consumer brand (approachable, soft)

## Translate answers into eliminations

The answers rule out territory immediately. Say what's now OFF the table — this is reassuring and focusing. Examples:

- "Warm recognition + indie maker" → eliminates cold AI-lab aesthetics (dark mode, neon, monospace-everything), generic ed-tech (mascots, candy colors, bubbly buttons), corporate SaaS gradients.
- "Calm & credible + research lab" → eliminates playful whimsy, hand-drawn, bright multi-color.

Then state the positive direction the answers point toward, and move to Step 1 to test it against real references.

## If the user has no vibe yet

Tell them directly: Step 1 will do more work than usual because they're discovering taste by reacting, not documenting a taste they already hold. Coach them to react fast — "yes / no / why" — and not overthink. Convergence comes from volume of quick reactions, not from one perfect answer.

## Accent color

If the user reaches for a specific color during reference reactions (e.g. they like one site's blue), surface it and ask whether to commit or explore options in Step 2. Committing early is fine and speeds everything up; the system can always widen later.
